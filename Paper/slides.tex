%% SLIDE 1: HIGH-LEVEL OVERVIEW
%% ============================================================
\begin{frame}{Overview}

This study examines the small firm not as a \emph{borrower}, but as a \emph{lender}---a role that small businesses frequently played in the U.S.\ before the rise of bank credit cards, and one they continue to play worldwide

\vfill

\textbf{Why it matters for policy}:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Merchants pay 2--4\% interchange fees on every card transaction
    \item Regulators worldwide debating whether to cap these fees (EU, UK, US Credit Card Competition Act)
    \item But, bank cards replaced even more expensive in-house credit operations
\end{itemize}

\vfill

\textbf{Main finding}: Bank credit cards allowed small firms to \emph{outsource} the credit supply function, \emph{reducing} their costs net of interchange fees

\end{frame}


%% ============================================================
%% SLIDE 2: ROADMAP
%% ============================================================
\begin{frame}{Roadmap}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Historical Context}: How store credit worked and why banks replaced it
    \vfill
    \item \textbf{Aggregate Trends}: Banks displaced stores as credit suppliers since 1958
    \vfill
    \item \textbf{Causal Evidence}: 1978 Supreme Court shock to bank card supply
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Store lending fell, firm profits and entry increased
    \end{itemize}
    \vfill
    \item \textbf{Mechanisms}: Cost reduction (labor, risk) dominates markup compression
    \vfill
    \item \textbf{Policy Implications}: Interchange fees vs.\ in-house credit costs
\end{enumerate}

\end{frame}

%% SLIDE 6: IDENTIFICATION STRATEGY
%% ============================================================
\begin{frame}{Identification: 1978 \emph{Marquette} Decision}

\begin{itemize}
    \item Supreme Court ruled banks can lend across state lines at \emph{home state's} usury rate
    \vfill
    \item States with usury $< 18\%$ in 1977 saw sudden increase in bank card supply from out-of-state banks
    \vfill
    \item 9 ``treated'' states (37M people, 16\% of nation)
    \vfill
    \item Revolving credit regulated separately---no effect on mortgages, business loans
    \vfill
    \item I do \emph{not} use the subsequent endogenous deregulation decisions: only the unexpected Supreme Court shock for identification
\end{itemize}

\end{frame}

%% SLIDE 10: NET ENTRY --- TRIPLE DIFF
%% ============================================================
\begin{frame}{Net Entry: Triple-Difference by Industry}

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Why net entry?} Accounting profits are a flawed proxy for economic profits and are observed only for the largest firms (Compustat); net entry captures the competitive response of the whole market.
\item \textbf{Why triple-difference?} Entrepreneurs also use credit cards to \emph{fund} their businesses. The triple-difference isolates the \emph{payment} channel: clothing retail had the highest card payment intensity, independent of card use by owners as borrowers.
\end{itemize}

\begin{frame}{The Model in One Picture}

\small Each face is a \textbf{retailer}; all retailers compete for customers, but \emph{harder} against rivals that accept the \textbf{same payment method} --- the grouped outlines below.

\vspace{0.2em}
\begin{center}
\includegraphics[width=0.72\textwidth]{Static/dudes.png}
\end{center}
\vspace{0.1em}

\begin{itemize}\setlength\itemsep{0.05em}
    \item Boxes = card-acceptance \textbf{nests}; stores inside a nest are closer substitutes.
    \item \textbf{Marquette} reshapes the nesting structure: as bank cards spread, more stores join the accepting nest (top row $\to$ bottom row).
    \item Calibration asks: \textbf{how much does the nest matter for where consumers shop?} That is what $\sigma$ measures.
\end{itemize}

\end{frame}


%% ============================================================
%% SLIDE 11c: HOW WE CALIBRATE — SIMULATE MARQUETTE, MATCH DiD
%% ============================================================
\begin{frame}{Calibrating to the \emph{Marquette} Experiment}

\small
Nested logit: \quad $u_{ij} = \delta_j + \varsigma_{g(j)} + (1-\sigma)\varepsilon_{ij}$, \quad where $\delta_j = \alpha \, p_j + \xi_j$

\begin{itemize}\setlength\itemsep{0.1em}
    \item $\sigma$ = fraction of taste dispersion correlated \emph{within} card-acceptance groups
    \item $\sigma \to 1$: customers choose only on payment method, not what the store sells
    \item $\sigma = 0$: payment method irrelevant; choice is on price and idiosyncratic taste
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.4em}
\textbf{How I calibrate:} I \emph{simulate Marquette inside the model} --- raising consumer bank-card usage and merchant acceptance to match the data --- and pick $(\sigma,\zeta)$ so the model reproduces the \textbf{actual diff-in-diff effects of Marquette}, estimated across my 252 retail markets:

\vspace{0.2em}
\begin{enumerate}\setlength\itemsep{0.1em}
    \item the average sales response of treated firms ($Treated\times Post$), and
    \item the \emph{extra} sales response of firms already accepting bank cards ($Treated\times Post\times Accept$).
\end{enumerate}

\vspace{0.2em}
{\footnotesize $\alpha$ (price sensitivity) is calibrated from the literature; only $\sigma$ and $\zeta$ (the cost change) are matched. Two moments, two parameters --- exactly identified.}

\end{frame}

%% SLIDE 14: CONCLUSION + POLICY
%% ============================================================
\begin{frame}{Conclusion and Policy Implications}

\textbf{Summary of findings:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Bank cards displaced store credit, reducing merchants' labor and risk costs
    \item Net effect on retail industry: $+$4\% entry, $+$2\% profits, better recession survival
    \item Cost reduction ($\sim$4\%) dominates markup compression (5bp)
\end{itemize}

\vfill

\textbf{Implications for interchange fee regulation:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Interchange fees replaced even higher in-house credit costs
    \item Small merchants benefit most from outsourcing credit to banks
    \item Capping fees may force merchants back toward costly in-house credit
    \item Historical evidence: the \emph{net} effect of card networks on merchants was positive
\end{itemize}

\end{frame}
